RNNR Mobile App

Design Process

The Challenge

Design an app that will help users develop habits and accomplish their goals.

RNNR started as a social habit app that allowed users to tailor their goals and notifications. I changed the focus to running based on user feedback.

TOOLS USED:

  • Paper and Ink
  • Balsamiq
  • Sketch
  • Illustrator
  • Invision

Rapid Prototyping

I made quick progress by brainstorming and sketching the app. This allowed me to develop a paper prototype to test with users.

INSIGHTS:

  • The users found that the experience too cumbersome.
  • Too many options.
  • Too much typing.

REFOCUSING:

  • I pivoted and refocused the app towards runners.
  • Focusing on the problems of runners allowed me to design sharper solutions.
  • Enabled me to develop more features to help runners accomplish their goals.

Brainstorming

The new focus required me to generate ideas and features for the running app. The mind map process helped me determine features to design.

Competitive Analysis

There are many running apps and devices in the marketplace. I tested and analyzed a number mobiles apps to determine how to differentiate RNNR from the field.

INSIGHTS:

  • Most apps have freemium models that only allow users to plan their goals if they pay for a membership.
  • Need to differentiate app.

ACTIONS:

  • Target the middle market (Runtastic) instead of the mass market (Fitbit, Nike+) and niche market for hard core athletes (Strava). Maintain free model.
  • Implement advertising business model (highly targeted: races, gear, and rewards).
Competitors
Overview Fitbit sells a variety of devices that track heart rate, steps, and distance. They have 9.5m active users. Runstactic is a popular running app with 180m app downloads and 90m+ registered users. Strava is an exercise tracking app targeting competitive athletes. They have 192,000 premium users and 1.2 active users.
Target Market Mass marketing including non-runners. Everyday runners. Serious athletes that want to analyze their performance: cylists, runners, and triathletes.
Key Features
  • Easy to use devices that can track heart rate, steps, sleep patterns, food consumption, and weight.
  • GPS is limited to high end devices. Fitbit mobile app allows GPS tracking
  • Achievements
  • Create and search for routes (Premium)
  • Cheer runners during their run (Premium)
  • Story running (running related audium books for purchase)
  • Training calendar and log
  • Flyby (Premium): track runs and rides with your group.
  • Route search and planning
  • Monthly challenges.
  • Lots of data: elevation, temperature, grade adjusted pace, etc.
Business Model
  • Hardware
  • Premium subscription: $49.99/year
  • Freemium
  • Puschase individual running plans
  • Premium Member: $9.00 / month or $49.00 / year
  • Freemium
  • Premium Membership: $59 / year
Strengths
  • Easy to use
  • Fun
  • Lots of features and resources (on website)
  • Connects with Fitbit.
  • Supports most GPS devices
  • Route planning
  • Training analysis
Weaknesses
  • Limited features without membership
  • Low accuracy without GPS input
 Limited stats with free version. Limited mobile app - doesn’t contain log, plan and run entry.

Developing Personas

Developed user personas based on user interviews to guide design decisions.

INSIGHTS:

  • Potential range of motivators and goals
  • Need to give users ability to adjust engagement

ACTIONS:

  • Align features towards runners that want to train for races. But don't overwhelm users during first time use and onboarding.
  • Allow users to adjust notifications, reminders, and nudges.

Creating User Flows

I started this stage of the process by developing user stories:

  • The user wants to set a running goal.
  • The user want to create a challenge for herself and her friends.
  • The user want to record a run.
  • The user wants to share her run with her friends.
  • The user wants to review and edit her running plan.
  • The user wants to check out her friends' activities.
  • Then I created flow charts to map the user's actions to accomplish her goals.

Usability Testing and User Feedback

I developed a prototype in Invision to test with users.

INSIGHTS:

  • Simplify UI. Prioritize information.
  • Remove map and icons when they're not needed.
  • Add function to change music.
  • Add A Run button needs to be more prominent.

INSIGHTS:

  • Re-drafted wireframes and user flows.
  • Removed background image.
  • Increased font size and removed borders.

Also, I loved the whimsical illustrations of Chris Fernandez and Anton Fritsler. They're fun and happy. This style can differentiate the app from the other running apps that are currently available.

ACTIONS:

  • Created style guide
  • Created icons and illustrations

prototype

NEXT STEPS:

  • Develop business model to determine viability in the marketplace

grax

Design Process

The Challenge

Enable people to support independent creators and journalists when they are motivated.

The idea came to me while listening to a podcast as I was walking my dogs. I thought about supporting the podcast but I couldn't do it at that moment. Of course, I had forgotten about it by the time I got home.

RNNR started as a social habit app that allowed users to tailor their goals and notifications. I changed the focus to running based on user feedback.

Initial Development

grax (formerly TinyTips) was initially developed as a browser extension during a hackathon. I collaborated with engineers to develop a working prototype. I created the wireframes and low-level mock-ups during the Hackathon. Also, I conducted preliminary research on competitors. We were under time constraints but we were able to develop a working demo within 5 hours.

TOOLS USED:

  • Paper and Ink
  • Sketch

Wireframes

I created the initial wireframes to include the sign-up experience.

INSIGHTS:

Designing in a hackathon posed unexpected challenges. My partners had to learn HTML and Javascript on the spot. So the development took longer than expected. Unfortunately, we were only able to create a demo of the tipping screen and couldn't get user feedback.

Post Hackathon Strategy

I was excited to continue developing the app after the hackathon.

The Plan:

  • Analyze competitive landscape and business model: PayPal, Patreon, Venmo, others
  • Survey creators and users.
  • Design web experience: user stories, wire framing, testing, and prototyping.

Competitive Analysis

Developing a solid business model is an important step it the product's development due to the cost of transaction fees. Also, services like PayPal already exist with large a large base customers (users) and businesses (creators).

I researched and tried to answer these key questions:

  • What's the competitive landscape?
  • How do you minimize the transaction fees?
  • What are the opportunities to monetize?
  • What's the market potential for this service?

FINDINGS AND INSIGHTS (but more questions):

  • Lots of successful (Patreon, PayPal) and unsuccessful (ProTipp, Reddcoin) competitors including. YouTube also has a Fan Funding feature that allows viewers to support creators.
  • Patreon bundles contributions and sends one transaction a month to minimize fees.
  • Monetization: take a cut of the contributions (Patreon = 5%, Paypal = 2.9%). Can we earn interest the moneys as we wait to bundle the contributions?
  • Potential market: the success of Patreon indicates an interest in supporting creators. Is there room for competition? What would be the competitive advantage or differentiator?

Sketching

Tipping a street performer is a physical act. The supporter pulls out a coin, walks up to the performer, and drops the coin into the hat. The supporter may notice the sound that the coin makes as it lands into the pile.

How do you replicate a similar experience in a tipping app?

I sketched animations for the coin to simulate the effect and give the user see the tipping process. Also, I wanted to give the user a option to give multiple times in one instance. The browser should reset enabling the user to tip again.

INSIGHTS:

Designing in a hackathon posed unexpected challenges. My partners had to learn HTML and Javascript on the spot. So the development took longer than expected. Unfortunately, we were only able to create a demo of the tipping screen and couldn't get user feedback.

Action:

  • Test 2 hover animation with users:
    • Add shadow
    • Enlarge
  • Test 3 on click animations with users:
    • Deposit
    • Drop
    • Spin

Conclusion

After analyzing the competitive landscape, I decided not to continue with grax.
